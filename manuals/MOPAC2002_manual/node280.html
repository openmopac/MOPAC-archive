RAPID

When only part of a geometry is being modified, the speed of the calculation can be increased by the use of RAPID. Thus, if a side-chain on a protein is being optimized, all atoms on the side-chain would be flagged with " 1 "s, and all other atoms would be flagged with " 0 "s. An atom is considered as being modified if any one of the three flags is " 1 ". In order for RAPID to work, all atoms that move must be flagged for optimization. Thus, if a single backbone atom, near the middle of a protein chain, is flagged for optimization, and no other atoms are flagged for optimization, RAPID should not be used, because many atoms that are not marked for optimization will move as a result of the flagged atom moving. One way to ensure that all atoms that move are flagged is to put all the flagged atoms at the end of the data-set. This is not essential, but it does prevent unflagged atoms from moving. To do this in Cartesian coordinates is simple. In internal coordinates, flag the desired atoms, convert to Cartesian coordinates, move the flagged atoms to the end of the data set, then convert back to internal coordinates. The effort to set up a system with RAPID is well worth while, particularly if only a few (up to 10-20%) of the atoms are flagged.
